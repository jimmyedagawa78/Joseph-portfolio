A Fulfilling Goodbye to a New Friend

By Joseph Blessing Stephen · 2 min read · 2021

What started as a casual watch of the Eva rebuild movies led to a 4-day binge of the entire Eva franchise — and took me on a rollercoaster I wasn't ready for.

Neon Genesis Evangelion is one of those series that has a reputation so large it precedes itself. Going in, I knew it was considered a landmark of anime. What I didn't expect was how deeply personal and emotionally demanding it would be — not just as a story, but as an experience.

THE REBUILD AS A STARTING POINT

The rebuild films — You Are (Not) Alone, You Can (Not) Advance, You Can (Not) Redo, and the final Thrice Upon a Time — are a reimagining of the original series rather than a direct continuation. Starting there actually works surprisingly well as an introduction. The animation is stunning, the scope feels cinematic, and it eases you into the world of Evangelion with enough emotional weight to pull you back to the source.

After the rebuild films, going back to the original 1995 series felt like finding the raw, unfiltered version of everything the rebuilds were gesturing toward. Hideaki Anno's personal struggles are embedded in every frame — and that honesty is what makes it so resonant even decades later.

A GOODBYE WORTH HAVING

Thrice Upon a Time serves as a final farewell to a franchise that has meant so much to so many people. Without spoiling what happens, it manages to be both a satisfying conclusion and a genuinely emotional send-off — the kind of ending that feels earned.

Evangelion doesn't ask you to understand it. It asks you to feel it.

For anyone who hasn't yet experienced Eva — start with the rebuilds, then go back to the series. Let it take you somewhere uncomfortable. It's worth every bit of it.
